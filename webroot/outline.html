Andrea Capecci

Outline - About Me

Unmethodical, informal, inimitable Andrea Capecci’s way of painting goes through a lot of experiments.
Starting from the industrial and environmental design, through advertising graphics for the world of fashion he lands to the art of painting.
“Denim” is the “fil rouge” of his personal artistic growth as he himself always says: “the rolls of denim, thrown in a corner, have stirred up my hidden passion for painting”.
Capecci’s relationship with the world of fashion is something like “odi et amo”.
He wants to change those industrial produces into something special, unique and inimitable. The artist uses personal techniques to handle denim: he puts his rolls of material on the ground, even one on the other in a casual way , colouring them through a “dripping” process which lets spots of colours fall on them or compactly or struggly , sometimes in holes or tears hidden in the falls of the denim itself.
So Capecci expresses his deep necessity of loosing any contact with things and time in a world where his everyday living may become less and less dull and plain.
The next evolution of Capecci’s art is his going back to the automatism of modern industry: he delivers his denim to the chemical washing and remains anxious spectator in front of the portholes of a laundry. Consequently every “denim” is a mystery and the final result is unknown and unique.
The question is: does a work of art remain unique and not repeatable even if it’s technically perfectly reproducible?
As Walter Benjamin says every work of art has got his own “aura” that is something the painter hands on and witnesses as not repeatable because conceived and created hic and nunc.
Looking at Capecci’s work one can clearly understand the influences of the past and the present times both in the paintings hang on the wall and in the ones like tapestry.
Therefore the observer is always transferred into other worlds where there are infinite realities.
He mingles his colours such in an original and striking way that he can be compared to Burri and Pollock and even to the American expressionism of Rothko.
The dualism between coincidence and willness can be observed in all his works as an interesting and peculiar element of them.
The more so Capecci will follow the fatality of his creations even less he will be compelled to control every single step of his painting story. The final result will be less and less uncertain giving him the chance to reach the total control of his different techniques and even of the fatality.
